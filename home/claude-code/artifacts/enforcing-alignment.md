# Enforcing alignment in software engineering: from documents to proofs

**The most durable alignment mechanisms in software engineering are those encoded as executable, automatically verified artifacts — not those that depend on human discipline.** This is the central finding across five decades of research into how teams maintain goal alignment, value alignment, and correctness enforcement. Architecture tests that fail a build, type systems that reject invalid programs, and fitness functions that block deployments reliably enforce intent over years, while design documents decay within months and cultural norms erode with each personnel change. The practical implication is stark: organisations that invest primarily in documentation and process compliance — rather than in making constraints machine-checkable — systematically lose architectural coherence as they scale. What follows is a deep examination of the theoretical frameworks and mechanisms humans have developed to solve this problem, ordered roughly from softest to hardest enforcement.

## Explicit artifacts: why most written alignment decays

Architecture Decision Records, design documents, and style guides represent the most widespread attempt to externalise design intent into durable artifacts. Their theoretical lineage runs through Parnas and Clements' 1985 paper "A Rational Design Process: How and Why to Fake It," which argued that while a perfectly rational design process is unachievable, the *documents* such a process would produce remain enormously valuable. Kruchten, Lago, and van Vliet (2006) later reframed architecture itself as a set of design decisions — elevating rationale capture to a first-class concern.

**ADRs**, popularised by Michael Nygard in 2011, are the lightest-weight expression of this idea: short Markdown files recording context, decision, and consequences. Their adoption is growing but fragile. Buchgeher et al.'s 2023 mining study of GitHub repositories found that roughly **50% of repos containing ADRs had only one to five records**, suggesting widespread try-then-abandon dynamics. Ahmeti et al.'s 2024 action research confirmed ADRs improved team cooperation but struggled with cross-service decisions in microservice architectures. Keeling (2022, IEEE Software) identified a subtler value: ADRs nudge developers toward architectural thinking by requiring them to articulate trade-offs. But the fundamental limitation remains — ADRs are not automatically enforceable. No CI pipeline rejects a commit because an ADR was not updated.

Design documents and RFCs face the same decay problem at greater scale. Google's design doc culture, described in *Software Engineering at Google* (Winters, Manshreck, and Wright, 2020), forces early identification of design issues when changes are still cheap and scales senior engineer knowledge across thousands of contributors. But Twitter's internal study of 50+ engineers found that their RFC template had bloated to 14+ pages, with most sections perceived as superfluous. Uber's evolution from DUCKs to RFCs to Engineering Review Docs (documented by Gergely Orosz) shows that even well-resourced companies must repeatedly redesign their document processes to combat noise and template fatigue.

**Style guides occupy a unique position** on the enforcement spectrum because they can be partially automated. Google's experience with `gofmt` — Go's canonical formatter — is instructive: once formatting became a compiler-adjacent tool, all debate about style vanished and "the cost of adherence fell dramatically." An engineer at Agoda submitted a 500-line pull request that received 140 comments, roughly 80% about style; after adding Scalafmt, the problem disappeared entirely. The pattern is clear: formatting rules enforced by tools achieve near-perfect compliance, syntactic patterns enforced by linters achieve strong compliance, but semantic conventions and design principles remain dependent on human judgment. As the *Software Engineering at Google* chapter on style guides puts it, rules must "pull their weight" — the measure of "too many" is not the raw number but how many an engineer must remember.

The most enforceable artifact-based mechanism is the **architecture fitness function**, introduced by Ford, Parsons, and Kua in *Building Evolutionary Architectures* (2017). Borrowing from evolutionary computing, fitness functions provide objective integrity assessments of architectural characteristics — performance budgets, dependency rules, security constraints — implemented as automated tests in CI/CD pipelines. They differ from unit tests by operating at the architectural level, protecting quality attributes rather than functional correctness. AWS published guides for implementing fitness functions using CloudWatch and Security Hub. The concept is powerful but faces a well-known limitation: the architectural properties that matter most (maintainability, evolvability) are precisely those hardest to reduce to automated metrics.

## Process enforcement: automated gates and their adversaries

The strongest process-based enforcement mechanisms are fully automated quality gates in CI/CD pipelines. But the history of these mechanisms reveals a persistent tension between enforcement rigour and the human capacity to game metrics — a dynamic best captured by Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

**Code review** is the oldest formal enforcement mechanism, originating with Fagan's 1976 IBM inspections, which reported finding **80–90% of defects** at a fraction of the cost of post-release fixes. But Bacchelli and Bird's landmark 2013 ICSE study at Microsoft overturned a key assumption: modern tool-based code reviews catch far fewer bugs than expected. Reviews primarily serve knowledge transfer, team awareness, and code understanding. The review *gate* (requiring approval before merge) is automated, but the review *substance* depends entirely on human attention. Failure modes are well-documented: rubber-stamping under deadline pressure, reviewer fatigue on diffs exceeding 400 lines, social dynamics where junior reviewers defer to senior contributors, and the common degeneration into style nitpicking rather than architectural scrutiny.

**Static analysis** has deeper roots than most practitioners realise. Stephen Johnson's original `lint` for C (1978, Bell Labs) established the paradigm but also its central challenge — false positives. Bessey et al.'s 2010 CACM paper "A Few Billion Lines of Code Later," reporting on Coverity's industrial deployment, confirmed that false positive management determines adoption success. NIST's Static Analysis Tool Exposition (SATE V, SP 500-326) found that no single tool catches all weakness types and that **tool precision varies enormously** — some reaching 90% for specific categories, others performing far worse. The practical consequence is that teams must carefully tune tool configurations per project, and that running multiple complementary tools provides the best coverage.

**Quality gates** operationalise static analysis and other checks as pipeline-blocking conditions. SonarQube's "Clean as You Code" methodology exemplifies the modern approach: enforce zero new issues, require minimum coverage on new code (default 80%), and demand review of all security hotspots — but apply these standards only to changed code, leaving legacy debt unaddressed. A 2024 study across five projects found bug reductions up to 100% and technical debt reduction from ~150 to ~69 hours over six weeks. But the gaming behaviours are equally well-documented: developers write trivial tests to inflate coverage numbers, suppress warnings wholesale when false positive rates are high, and exclude files from analysis to inflate metrics. Hora's 2025 study of Python projects specifically examined code exclusion practices.

The **test coverage debate** illustrates the limits of metric-based enforcement particularly well. The industry-standard 80% threshold lacks strong empirical justification. Inozemtseva and Holmes (2014, ICSE) found that when controlling for test suite size, **coverage is not strongly correlated with test suite effectiveness** — suggesting coverage may be primarily a proxy for test suite size rather than quality. Google's own large-scale study (Ivanković et al., 2019, ESEC/FSE) across a billion lines of code had not found a definitive correlation between coverage and defect rates. A case study on the Android platform found it "is not necessarily true that components with low coverage have more defects." Coverage remains useful as infrastructure for tooling (dependency analysis, change impact assessment) even if its direct quality signal is weaker than assumed.

**Architectural linting tools** represent the most promising intersection of automation and architectural enforcement. ArchUnit (Java), Dependency Cruiser (JavaScript/TypeScript), NDepend (.NET), and deptrac (PHP) encode architectural constraints as executable tests that run in CI/CD and block merges on violation. ArchUnit's layered architecture DSL — `layeredArchitecture().layer("Controller").definedBy("..controller..").whereLayer("Controller").mayNotBeAccessedByAnyLayer()` — turns informal architecture diagrams into continuously verified contracts. Xebia's experience report with Dependency Cruiser found that activating rules "uncovered numerous incorrect imports and misplaced files, even in our high-quality, relatively new React codebase" and that once enforced, "dependency errors were scarce... reinforcing that our design remains intact, averting the risk of incremental degradation, or, more starkly, death by a thousand cuts." Rosik et al.'s 2011 study provided the critical counterpoint: even when architectural inconsistencies were *detected* via Reflexion Modelling, detection alone "did not act as a trigger for their removal." Detection without enforcement is insufficient — the constraint must block the pipeline.

## Conway's law, team topologies, and the organisational dimension

No account of alignment in software engineering is complete without addressing the insight that **organisational structure and system architecture are not independent design problems — they are two projections of the same coordination system**. Melvin Conway's 1968 observation that organisations are "constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations" has been empirically validated more rigorously than almost any other claim in software engineering.

The most compelling evidence comes from three studies. MacCormack, Baldwin, and Rusnak's 2012 study in *Research Policy* exploited a natural experiment comparing functionally identical software built by tightly-coupled commercial firms versus loosely-coupled open-source communities, finding modularity differences up to **a factor of six** in propagation cost. Colfer and Baldwin's 2016 meta-study surveyed **142 empirical studies** across industries, finding the mirroring hypothesis prevalent but not universal — partial mirroring, where knowledge boundaries are drawn more broadly than operational boundaries, may be superior in dynamic environments. Most strikingly, Nagappan, Murphy, and Basili's 2008 study of Windows Vista at Microsoft found that **organisational metrics predicted failure-proneness with 85% precision and recall** — significantly higher than code metrics like complexity, churn, or coverage. Organisational structure was literally a better predictor of bugs than code quality.

**Team Topologies** (Skelton and Pais, 2019) operationalises these insights into a practical framework with four team types — stream-aligned, platform, enabling, and complicated-subsystem — and three interaction modes: collaboration, X-as-a-Service, and facilitating. The framework's central design principle is **cognitive load management**: team boundaries should be drawn so no single team must hold more complexity than it can effectively reason about. Collaboration is explicitly characterised as expensive and temporary; the steady-state goal is X-as-a-Service, where teams interact through well-defined contracts rather than high-bandwidth communication. This directly encodes Malone and Crowston's coordination theory (1994, ACM Computing Surveys), which defined coordination as "the process of managing dependencies among activities" and identified shared resources, producer/consumer relationships, and simultaneity constraints as the fundamental dependency types requiring coordination mechanisms.

The **Inverse Conway Maneuver** — coined by LeRoy and Simons in 2010, endorsed by Forsgren, Humble, and Kim in *Accelerate* (2018) — represents the proactive application: deliberately restructure teams to produce the desired architecture. Amazon's two-pizza teams with Bezos's API mandate (~2002) and Spotify's squad model (Kniberg and Ivarsson, 2012) are the canonical implementations. But Fowler cautions that if an existing system has rigid architecture, "changing the development organization isn't going to be an instant fix. Instead it's more likely to result in a mismatch between developers and code that adds friction." The existing architecture pushes back against new team structures; both must evolve together iteratively.

The **principal-agent problem** manifests acutely in software teams as the tension between feature velocity and architectural quality. When incentive structures reward shipping features quickly, developers — as rational agents — optimise for delivery speed at the expense of long-term system health. Ward Cunningham's technical debt metaphor directly encodes this dynamic. The resolution mechanisms identified in the literature include tight feedback loops connecting engineers to business outcomes, visible IC career ladders that reward architectural contribution, and ownership models (like two-pizza teams) where the team that builds a service also operates it and bears the consequences of its technical debt.

## Formal methods: the theoretical ceiling of correctness enforcement

Formal methods represent the strongest possible alignment mechanism — mathematical proof that a system satisfies its specification. The practical question has always been whether the cost is justified, and for which systems.

The foundational framework is **Hoare logic** (Hoare, 1969, CACM): the Hoare triple {P} S {Q} establishes that if precondition P holds before statement S executes, postcondition Q holds afterward. Bertrand Meyer operationalised this as **Design by Contract** in the Eiffel programming language (1986–1997), with `require` (preconditions), `ensure` (postconditions), and `invariant` (class invariants) as native language constructs checked at runtime. The subcontracting rules — subclasses may weaken preconditions and strengthen postconditions — approximate the Liskov Substitution Principle through a formal mechanism. DbC adoption remains limited primarily because most mainstream languages lack native support, relegating contracts to libraries or annotations with weaker guarantees.

**TLA+** at Amazon Web Services demonstrates that formal specification can be cost-effective at industrial scale. Newcombe et al.'s 2015 CACM paper reported that since 2011, seven AWS teams had used TLA+ to verify DynamoDB, S3, EBS, and internal lock managers. Engineers learned TLA+ "from scratch and got useful results in **two to three weeks**." The tool found subtle design errors in concurrent protocols that were virtually impossible to discover through testing — race conditions, edge cases in replication protocols, violations of consistency guarantees. Chris Newcombe stated: "TLA+ is the most valuable thing that I've learned in my professional career." A 2025 follow-up reported AWS also adopted the P programming language because many engineers found TLA+'s mathematical notation challenging despite its power.

**Type systems** provide the most pervasive formal enforcement mechanism in everyday programming, grounded in the **Curry-Howard correspondence** — the deep isomorphism between propositions and types, proofs and programs. Pierce's *Types and Programming Languages* (2002) defines a type system as "a syntactic method for automatically checking the absence of certain erroneous behaviors." Advanced type systems extend this dramatically: dependent types (Agda, Idris, Coq) allow types to depend on values, enabling encoding of arbitrary specifications; refinement types (Liquid Haskell, F*) augment standard types with logical predicates verified by SMT solvers. Rust's ownership and borrow checker represents perhaps the most practically successful type-level enforcement — eliminating use-after-free, double-free, dangling references, and data races at compile time with zero runtime overhead. Google's Chromium security team estimates that approximately **70% of serious security bugs** stem from memory safety issues, precisely the category Rust eliminates.

The gap between formal methods and industrial practice is real but narrowing. Davis et al.'s 2013 survey of nine US aerospace organisations identified **education as the primary barrier** (27 of all barrier responses), followed by tool usability ("not usable by mere mortals") and non-technical organisational factors. DeMillo, Lipton, and Perlis's provocative 1979 CACM paper argued that program proofs cannot play the same social role as mathematical proofs because they are too long for communal review. But the counterexamples have accumulated: the seL4 microkernel's complete functional correctness proof (Klein et al., 2010), CompCert's verified C compiler (Leroy, 2006), the Paris Métro Line 14 driverless train system verified with the B-Method (Behm et al., 1998), and IBM's CICS specification in Z notation that reportedly saved **$5 million**. Lightweight formal methods — Alloy's bounded model checking via SAT solving (Jackson, 2006), TLA+'s model checker — represent the pragmatic middle ground, trading completeness for full automation and immediate feedback.

## Organisational memory and the problem of knowledge that walks out the door

All enforcement mechanisms ultimately depend on the knowledge that designed them. When that knowledge is lost through attrition, the mechanisms themselves become misunderstood artifacts — maintained by habit rather than understanding, eventually circumvented or abandoned.

The scale of this problem is quantifiable. Avelino et al. and Cosentino et al.'s studies of 133 popular GitHub projects found that **65% of systems have a bus factor of two or fewer** — meaning two departures could incapacitate the project. Walsh and Ungson's 1991 framework in the *Academy of Management Review* identified five "storage bins" for organisational memory: individuals, culture, transformations (processes), structures, and ecology (workspace). In software teams, these map to developer expertise, team norms, CI/CD pipelines, team topology and repository organisation, and development environment configurations. The fragility is concentrated in the first bin — individual knowledge — which studies suggest accounts for **70–80% of a software organisation's total knowledge**, with only 20–30% externalised into documents and code.

Nonaka and Takeuchi's SECI model (1995) provides the theoretical framework for understanding knowledge conversion: socialisation (tacit-to-tacit, via pair programming and apprenticeship), externalisation (tacit-to-explicit, via design documents and ADRs), combination (explicit-to-explicit, via knowledge bases), and internalisation (explicit-to-tacit, via learning-by-doing). Polanyi's foundational insight from *The Tacit Dimension* (1966) — "we can know more than we can tell" — establishes a hard limit: some knowledge cannot be fully externalised regardless of documentation effort. Grant's 2007 review of 60 knowledge management papers found Polanyi's work frequently misinterpreted as a binary dichotomy rather than a continuous spectrum.

**Transactive memory systems** (Wegner, 1987) describe how teams develop shared awareness of "who knows what" — a metamemory directory enabling specialisation, information routing, and retrieval coordination. Lewis (2003, 2004) operationalised this with a measurement scale across three dimensions: specialisation, credibility, and coordination. Research on software teams found that well-developed transactive memory systems help teams withstand member loss, though effectiveness depends on which member is lost.

The most promising response is **living documentation** — Cyrille Martraire's 2019 framework arguing that documentation should evolve at the same pace as code. Executable specifications (Cucumber/Gherkin, FIT/FitNesse) embody this principle: specifications verified by execution cannot go stale undetected. The "architecture as code" movement extends this to structural constraints: ArchUnit tests are simultaneously enforcement mechanisms and documentation — new developers learn architectural rules from the test suite itself. Simon Brown's C4 model and Structurizr tool address diagram decay by generating architecture visualisations from code-defined models stored in version control. Policy as Code (Open Policy Agent, HashiCorp Sentinel) applies the same principle to governance rules.

But documentation decay is relentless. Research from Readme found technical documentation becomes materially outdated within **30–90 days**; Zoomin found 68% of enterprise technical content hadn't been updated in over six months. Lehman's Laws of Software Evolution (1985) predict this: "when a program is modified, its complexity will increase, provided that one does not actively work against it." The same entropy applies to documentation. Only documentation that is *executed* — tests, fitness functions, architecture checks, type signatures — resists this decay, because divergence between specification and implementation manifests as a failing build rather than a quietly misleading wiki page.

## Conclusion: the enforcement hierarchy and its implications

The mechanisms surveyed here form a clear hierarchy of enforcement durability. At the base sit cultural mechanisms — shared taste, implicit norms, verbal agreements — which erode fastest under personnel change and organisational growth. Above these sit documented artifacts — ADRs, design documents, style guides — which decay within months without active curation. Process-based mechanisms — code review requirements, definitions of done — persist longer but remain vulnerable to rubber-stamping and social pressure.

The durable mechanisms are those where **the constraint is the artifact**: type systems where an invalid program does not compile, architecture tests where a forbidden dependency fails the build, fitness functions where a performance regression blocks deployment, formal specifications where a protocol violation is caught by a model checker. Perry and Wolf's 1992 distinction between architectural *erosion* (violation of principles) and *drift* (insensitivity to architecture) maps directly onto this hierarchy — soft mechanisms allow both, while executable constraints prevent erosion even as they cannot fully prevent drift in unmeasured dimensions.

Three insights emerge that cut across all five domains. First, **detection without enforcement is insufficient** — Rosik et al.'s 2011 finding that identifying architectural inconsistencies did not trigger their removal is the empirical anchor for this claim. Second, **every metric-based gate is subject to Goodhart's Law** — coverage thresholds produce trivial tests, complexity limits produce unnecessary decomposition, and quality gates produce warning suppression, necessitating defence-in-depth with diverse, complementary mechanisms rather than reliance on any single metric. Third, **organisational structure is itself an enforcement mechanism** — Nagappan et al.'s finding that organisational metrics outperform code metrics as bug predictors suggests that how teams are drawn may matter more than what tools they use. The strongest alignment posture combines the Inverse Conway Maneuver (structuring teams to produce desired architecture), executable architectural constraints (ArchUnit, fitness functions, type systems), and formal methods where the cost of failure justifies the cost of proof — a layered defence where each mechanism covers the gaps left by others.
